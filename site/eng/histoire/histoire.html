History

Founding of Kyoto - 794

In 794, Emperor Kanmu decided to move the capital to a new location: he founded the city of Heian-kyō, which would later become Kyoto.
The aim: to escape the religious influence of Nara (the former capital) and have a more stable capital.

Heian period - 1192

A cultural golden age marked by the flourishing of literature, the arts, and Buddhism. The emperor resided in Heian-kyō (present-day Kyoto), the country's political and cultural center.
In 1185, the Minamoto clan triumphed over the Taira at the battle of Dan-no-ura. In 1192, Minamoto no Yoritomo became shogun, marking the end of the Heian period and the beginning of samurai power.

Political decline but cultural prestige - 1192 to 1600 - Kamakura, Muromachi, Azuchi-Momoyama periods

Power is passed to the shoguns, often located elsewhere (Kamakura, then Edo). Kyoto remained the city of the emperor and of culture.
It experienced conflicts such as the Ōnin War, but also great artistic wealth (Zen art, Noh theater, gardens, etc.).

Edo era - 1600 to 1868

The Tokugawa shogunate establishes its headquarters in Edo (present-day Tokyo), which becomes the center of power.
Kyoto remains symbolically important: the residence of the emperor, a religious and cultural center.
It became a traditional city, with a refined lifestyle (geishas, tea ceremonies, etc.).

Meiji Restoration - 1868 to 20th century

In 1868, Emperor Meiji left Kyoto for Tokyo, which became the new capital. Kyoto lost its political role, but entered an era of gradual modernization.
It retains a strong cultural heritage.

Modernization and heritage conservation
(20th century to today)

Kyoto was spared bombing during the Second World War because of its valuable cultural heritage (the American Secretary of War, Henry L. Stimson, had visited Kyoto before the war and knew of its historical importance. He reportedly personally insisted that the city be removed from the target list). This helped preserve its major historic sites, such as Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion), Kiyomizu-dera (Kiyomizu Temple), and many others.

After the war, Kyoto began a process of restoration. Important sites were renovated, and the city became a model of heritage preservation in Japan.

At the same time, Kyoto implemented policies designed to protect its historic districts, traditional gardens, and ancient architecture, while avoiding the massive industrialization affecting other major cities in the country.

In 1994, Kyoto was listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its 17 historic sites of worldwide importance, which can be viewed on the monuments page .
